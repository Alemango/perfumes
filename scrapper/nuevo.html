Santal or sandalwood in far-eastern (China, Japan and Korea) conception is usually associated with tranquility, meditation and introspection due to its calming and soothing effcetwoody with a distinctive creamy touch. Of course the sandalwood aforementioned is limited to Santalum album (Indian sandalwood, Mysore sandalwood or other names). Another fact is, the wood chunks harvested from aged trees, say 60 years old, vary too. Heart wood close to root or ground is of the best quality with slightly darker colour and creamier woody scent. Yet wood adjacent to the bark part has its value too with lighter and more saw-dust-like scent. But please be kindly noted, either requires a proper aging process to achieve its scent of prime. A perfect example is Tam Dao EDP by Diptyque, for me, the consecutive top, heart and base notes capture exactly the layers of scent from bark to heart, as time passes it evolves from dry and cedar-like to the creamy sweet woody aura.

It is commonly understood a perfume is not an imitation but an interpretation of the artist's observation and thoughts of nature, the conclusion certainly should apply to Santal 33. However, I personally dislike it because it reveals less sandalwood than other elements, namely papyrus and leather. If you are not familiar with the former, try to pick up the green and herbaceous yet woody characters, that is papyrus; while the latter is self-explanatorybirch tar, leather bags, even rubber to someone's nose.
